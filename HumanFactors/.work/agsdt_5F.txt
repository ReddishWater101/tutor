Serial search model (Conjunction search)

 Search for target, inspect an object and make a decision, and then move on
 The idea is simple: You look at one object, decide if it's the target or not, and

  then move on to the next object
 When searching for target...

      T = average time to find target
      N = total number of items in search field
      I = average inspection time for each item (assume constant)
      On average you go through at least half items in visual field to find target (divide total field

        search time by 2)

                                    = 2

  target detection requires

 Conspicuity is how well targets
 stand out

 Promotes bottom-up processing
 (grabbing attention)

 Allows targets to be scanned in
 parallel (as opposed to in series;
 e.g., common coloring)

 Compromised when all targets are
 made to stand out!

 "Feature Search", "Disjunctive

Properties affecting conspicuity

 Discriminability from background elements:

     Coloring ­ Non-target (i.e., noise) items are uniformly colored
     Size ­ Target is larger
     Shape ­ Target is different
     Brightness ­ Target is brighter
     Motion ­ Background (i.e., noise) is stationary

 Simplicity ­ Defining targets in terms of one feature (e.g., color=red) vs.
 multiple features (e.g., color=red and size=small)

Signal detection theory

 Formal model of information and decision-making criteria
 Allows for optimal decision-making under certain conditions
 Provides a model of factors affecting human decision-making

 behavior

 History:

     Original developed in late 1940s for radar detection problems
     Extended to human perception and decision-making in 1960s
     Used for complex human/automation alerting problems in the 1990s

Signal detection theory

 States of reality ­ Noise or Signal + Noise

     Categories of response ­ "Yes" or "No"

 Four possible outcomes in SD task:

     Hit (H)
     False alarm (FA)
     Miss (M)
     Correct rejection (CR)

 High sensitivity = high likelihood of H + CR
 Low sensitivity = high likelihood of FA + Miss

Sensitivity (discriminability)

 How hard / easy it is for observer to discriminate
  signal from noise

 Not influenced by response criterion; influenced
  by degree of separation between two
  distributions (noise and signal+noise)

      Statistical distance between means of two distributions

 Greater separation = greater sensitivity

 Affects bottom-up processing ­ ability to detect
  signal based on features (e.g., brightness,
  loudness)

 Compromised by operator state - A fatigued
  operator is less sensitive; less capable of
  distinguishing signal from noise

Response criterion shift

 Example: If field intelligence indicated
 that a terrorist had threatened a flight,
 thus increasing the likelihood of a threat
 object, the screener would be more likely
 to regard an alarmed bag as a threat
 object. That is, as the probability of a
 signal increases, the response criterion is
 lowered (reducing ), so that anything
 remotely suggesting a signal (a threat
 object in our example) might be called a
 signal (Sanders & McCormick, 1987).
