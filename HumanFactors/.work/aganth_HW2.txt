Part B: Short Answer (27 pts)

   1. Define static (structural) anthropometry and dynamic (functional) anthropometry. Give
       one example of each and its use in workplace design. (4 points)
       Static anthropometry: measurement of body dimensions in standard, still postures
       (e.g., sitting height, stature). Example: designing chair height using popliteal height.

       Dynamic anthropometry: measurement of dimensions during movement or activity
       (e.g., forward reach, overhead reach). Example: designing control panel reach dis-
       tances.

   2. List three factors that affect anthropometric data. Briefly explain their impact. (6
       points)
       1) Age ­ stature increases until early adulthood, then decreases with age.
       2) Gender ­ men generally larger in most dimensions; women larger in hip/thigh area.
       3) Posture/clothing ­ slumping decreases stature, clothing/shoes increase external di-
       mensions.
       4) Others: e.g., ethnicity...

   3. What is the Morant technique? When might it be used? (2 points)
       A method using perpendicular grids to project body landmarks for measurement. Use-

       ful for capturing accurate anthropometric data in research or when direct caliper mea-
       surement is impractical.

   4. Describe various anthropometric design principles. Provide an example for each design
       type. (10 points)
       1) Design for the mean (50th percentile). Example: desk height in classrooms.
       2) Design for one extreme (small user, 5th percentile). Example: reach controls in
       cockpits.
       3) Design for the other extreme (large user, 95th percentile). Example: doorway
       clearance.
       4) Design for adjustability. Example: adjustable office chairs, car seats.
       5) Design for various sizes. Example: The design of clothing to fit people from diverse
       statures.

   5. A worker has a stature of 177 cm. Using correlations, estimate whether their arm reach
       is likely to be above or below average. Briefly explain why correlations between body
       dimensions matter in anthropometry. (3 points)
       Since stature and arm reach are positively correlated, a worker with above-average
       stature (177 cm) is likely to have above-average arm reach. Correlations allow esti-
       mation of related body dimensions when only one measure (like stature) is available,
       improving design predictions.

   6. Explain the concept of radius of gyration. How is it different from center of mass? (2
       points)
       The radius of gyration (k) is the distance from the axis of rotation at which the entire
       mass of a body segment could be assumed to be concentrated without changing its
       moment of inertia (MOI). It reflects how mass is distributed relative to an axis.
       The center of mass (CoM), on the other hand, is the point where the segment's mass
       is equally balanced in all directions.

       So, the key difference is that CoM locates the balance point of mass while radius of
       gyration describes the distribution of mass around an axis for rotational calculations.

   1. You are designing a control panel that requires both reach and clearance. Which
       dimensions would you select for each, which percentiles would you use, and how would
       you balance conflicting needs? Provide sketches or a written justification. (10 points)
       Dimensions to select:
       Reach (functional): forward grip reach (standing/sitting), vertical grip reach, lateral
       reach, arm reach envelope based on upper + lower arm link lengths; preferred vs. max-
       imum reach arcs.
       Clearance (structural): shoulder breadth (standing), elbow-to-elbow breadth (sitting),
       hip breadth (sitting) for passage/side clearances, popliteal height & thigh clearance
       (under-panel/knee space), sitting/standing eye height (sight lines), hand breadth/thickness

                                                          3
   (control spacing), head clearance if overhangs exist.

   Percentiles:
   Reach: design to the 5th percentile female so the smallest users can access primary
   controls without leaning/twisting.
   Clearance: design to the 95th percentile male (plus allowances for clothing/PPE) so
   the largest users fit without contact or constraint.

   Balancing conflicts: One is when 5th-percentile reach collides with 95th-percentile
   clearance:
   (1) Adjustability: Provide panel height/tilt adjustment or an adjustable seat/stand
   platform to shift the reach envelope while maintaining knee/thigh/head clearances.
   (2) Zoning: Place high-frequency, safety-critical controls within the preferred reach
   zone of the 5th-pct female; put low-frequency items in the extended zone.
   (3) Geometry: Use a slanted panel or stepped tiers to shorten vertical reach without
   stealing under-panel knee space.
   (4) Spacing: Size control spacing by 95th-pct hand breadth/thickness to prevent inad-
   vertent activation and accommodate gloves.
   (5) Aisles & edges: Set side/overhead clearances by 95th-pct shoulder breadth and
   head clearance with a margin. Principle: prioritize safety/clearance for the largest
   users, then recover small-user reach via adjustability and control zoning.
