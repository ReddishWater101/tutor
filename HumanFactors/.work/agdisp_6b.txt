 What are Orders of Control?

 Computer cursor control example:
     Mouse cursor position is controlled with rate (first -order) controller
     Mouse movement dictates rate/velocity of cursor motion:

              If velocity of control device (mouse) is high, high gain is applied to cursor displacement

              When control device moves slowly, low gain is applied (in some cases less than 1 for high

Control Principles

A. Attention

    1. Proximity compatibility : The same as proximity compatibility for displays.
    2. Avoid resource competition : When human are performing more than one

         task at the same time, multiple resource theory predicts a benefit of
         dividing the tasks across different mental resources.

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING                       14
Control Principles

B. Perceptual

     1. Make accessible : The controller should be
          easily reached.

     2. Make discriminable : Identifying a particular
          control from an array of controls requires that
          they are discriminable.

     3. Exploit redundancy gain : Redundancy gain can
          make controllers easier to identify and
          discriminate.

     4. Avoid absolute judgment limits : Absolute
          judgement limits govern how many types of
          controls can be differentiated by anyone type of
          code such as color to no more than five to
          seven (Note: combining codes can expand this
          number).

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING  15
Control Principles

C. Memory

    1. Knowledge in the world : The actuation of the control should be reflected in the
         control itself, such that the position of toggle switch indicates its state or when the
         illumination of the button indicates its state or when the illumination of indicate a
         system is on.

    2. Be consistent : Consistency makes it possible for people to apply skills from one
         situation to another, reducing errors and response time . Standardization should be
         considered for functions within a system as well as across systems (e.g. bad
         design could be location of light control between cars)

Control Principles

D. Mental Model

    1. Location compatibility : Control location should be close to the entity being
         controlled or the display of the entity.

    2. Movement compatibility : The direction of movement of a control should be
         congruent with direction both of movement of the displayed indicator and
         the system movement itself.

RS11. Avoid accidental activation

Control forcing functions:

 Constraint on control operation

 Intended to prevent certain behaviors (usually errors)
  that would otherwise damage system

      e.g., cannot shift automobile transmission from Park without
        depressing break

      e.g., cannot shift automobile transmission into reverse when
        vehicle is in motion

      e.g., putting weight on stairs causes the wheel lock to become
        engaged

RS15. Provide Feedback

 System response to control / user input

 Multiple sensory channels can receive feedback:

      Vision
      Audition
      Haptic sense

 Factors in real -time feedback effectiveness (e.g., warnings):

      Clarity of presentation
      Efficiency of presentation
      Degree of user annoyance
      User satisfaction

 Factors influencing use of feedback:

      Human perceptual processing time (>200 ms )
      Human information processing bandwidth
      Human anticipation of information content or control options
