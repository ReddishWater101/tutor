Seated Work Areas

 A seated posture:

      Improves feeling of balance
      Allows for reduced static load to maintain body postures
      Improves blood circulation
      Less fatigue development

 However, long periods of sitting on a poorly designed chair may lead to:

      Slackening of abdominal muscles
      Back and neck aches
      Curvature of the spine

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING              5
Standing Work Areas

 A standing posture:

      Greater physiological load, maintaining balance and posture
      May lead to blood and body fluid accumulation in legs (if not moving).

 So, which design is better standing or seated work areas?

SEATED vs STANDING

 Exercise: In each of the below cases, which type of workstation is more suitable:

1. Precise foot control actions are required.
2. Fine assembly or writing tasks are predominant.
3. There are frequent demands for downward force application (wrapping, packing).
4. Heavy objects are being handled [more than 4.5 kg (10 lb.) in weight].
5. No leg and knee clearance is available.
6. No large forces are required.
7. A high degree of body stability or equilibrium is required.
8. Mobility is a must.
9. Long work periods are expected.
10. All items needed within the task cycle can easily be supplied within the seated workspace.
11. High, low, or extended reaches are frequently required.

SEATED Work Areas

 Design considerations for seated work areas:

     Correct eye position relative to viewing tasks
     Adjustability of seat height.
     Adjustable of back rest (if small).
     Backrest width.
     Lumbar support provided.
     Seat surface depth and width.
     Seat surface sloped backward with a slight dip in

      the middle for extra body stability.
     Front edge of seat should be curving downwards.
     Cushioned seat.

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING  8
SEATED Work Areas (Cont'd)

 Design considerations for seated work areas:

     Clearance for the thighs and the knees

     Foot rests provided.

     Arm support for reducing intervertebral disk
      pressure. If trunk mobility is desired, armrests
      are not recommended; pads on work surface may
      be considered instead.

     Place larger and heavier objects closer to the
      operator.

     Work surface height depends on elbow height
      and the nature of the task.

     Multiple legs on seat for stability.

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING  9
STANDING Work Areas

 Design considerations for standing work areas:

     Proper monitor position with respect to NLS
     Reach distances
     Knee and foot clearance
     A smooth and level standing surface must be provided.
     There should be room for occasional moving around.
     A nonslip and padded standing surface (floor mat) is

       recommended.
     If two hand reaches are required, design for shorter

       reach distances.
     Work surface height depends on the task being

       performed.

Guiding principles of arrangement

 Importance principle . Components that are most important in the functioning of
  the system are located in the optimal locations. Rate each component's
  criticality in meeting system's objectives and assign locations accordingly.

 Sequence of use principle . Review sequential relationships between components
  under different tasks. Those always used one after the other would be located
  next to each other.

 Functional principle . Components that are functionally related to each other are
  grouped together. Then groups and components within groups can be arranged
  following other principles.

 Frequency of use principle . Components that are used most frequently are
  located in the most accessible/optimal areas.

Work Area Arrangements: Link Analysis

 Link analysis: an objective method of developing data for component arrangement.

      A sequential link of 5 for A B means that component B has been used five times right after
        component A.

      A functional link reports the number of times a component is used per unit of time or a task
        cycle.
