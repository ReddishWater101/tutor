Correlations of Body Measurements

 Some body dimensions are significantly correlated with each other, while some
  others are not.

 The higher the correlation coefficient between two body dimensions, the more
  closely related they are.

 Such data can be used to estimate one dimension from another.

Calculating Percentiles

 Given a set of raw data, the percentile(s)
 may be found by the following procedure:

     Find the mean
     Find the standard deviation (SD).
     Find the factor F1 (Z) corresponding to a

      specific percentile point. Multiply this factor
      by SD and add to (if above 50th percentile) or
      subtract from (if below 50th percentile) the
      mean.

Anthropometric Design Principles                                      - sensitive.

1. Design for the Mean
 Design for the mean (50th percentile) user.
 Useful when adjustability is not critical and the task isn't safety
 Risk: Doesn't accommodate users at the extremes.

Anthropometric Design Principles

2. Design for one extreme of spectrum (short or small user)

      Workplace design Ł reach is primary concern

3. Design for other extreme of spectrum (tall or large user)

      Workplace design Ł ingress and egress is primary concern

Anthropometric Design Principles

4. Design for adjustability
Provision for using mechanical solution to accommodate a range of users

 (the range from the 5th percentile female to the 95th percentile male)

 Design for adjustability:
     Consumer product Ł very common
     Workplaces Ł not that common

 Not practical or desirable in many design situations:

      Economically infeasible
      Design constraints

Anthropometry Analysis General Approach

 Step 1: Determine the user population (e.g., children, women, U.S. civilians, age
  groups, world populations, and races)

 Step 2: Determine relevant body dimensions (e.g., the design of a chair seat
  must consider waist breadth)

 Step 3: Select the design principles (what percentage of the population is to be
  accommodated)

 Step 4: Locate the anthropometric tables of the appropriate population.
 Step 5: Determine the percentage of population to be accommodated.
 Step 6: Make appropriate allowances for clothing (not an essential step)
 Step 7: Compute the specific dimension.
