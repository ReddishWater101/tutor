 History of the Field: First Appearances

 The foundations of the science of ergonomics appear to go back
  as far as humans do.

 Ancient tools (e.g., bows, levers) showed basic ergonomic
  principles.

 Hippocrates used ergonomic principles in his description of how
  a surgeon's workspace should be designed and how the tools
  should be arranged during surgery to maximize safety and
  efficiency (~400 BC).

 Over the centuries that followed, the effectiveness of tools such
  as hammers, axes and plows improved through changes in
  design and usage protocols, thereby increasing productivity.

                                                                    Hippocrates, Greek Physician

 History of the Field: First Appearances

 A major push for worker safety and health came during and
  after the industrial revolution (1760 Ł 1830 ) primarily in
  Europe .

 Earliest known indications of ergonomics are attributed to
  the Italian physician and philosopher Bernardino Ramazzini .

      Founder of occupational medicine, and his findings made an
        early case for ergonomics .

      In the 1700 s, he published "De Morbis Artificum Diatriba " (or
        "Diseases of Workers") Ł the first comprehensive work on
        occupational diseases and their associated causes .

 The term "ergonomics" was first coined in 1857 by
  Wojciech Jastrzebowski , an author and Polish scientist .

History of the Field: First Applications

 A few years later in 1883 , engineer Frederick Taylor applied                Frederick Winslow Taylor
  ergonomic principles to improve factory productivity .

       Often referred to as the "father of industrial engineering"

       He held the interests of the worker as highly as the interests of the
         owners .

       Focused on training workers to their maximum potential to increase
         overall system output .

       Employers were able to pay higher wages while still experiencing
         increased profit .

 Also, in the early 1900 s, Frank and Lillian Gilbreth conducted
  motion analysis studies that gave insight into the required
  movements and their associated times for a variety of
  common occupational actions .

       These became known as "therbligs"

World War I & II Ł Birth of Human Factors

 As early as WWI (1914 -1918 ), US and Great Britain,
  exercised selection and training, i.e., fitting the
  worker to the task .

 Soldiers struggled with poor equipment design . It
  was observed that complex military equipment
  could not be operated effectively or safely .

       About 400 aircrafts and many lives were lost in US due to
         use problems .

 Military scientists and psychologists began
  conducting research on humans in occupational
  settings .

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING     7
World War I & II Ł Birth of Human Factors

 It was not until WWII (1939 -1945 ) that ergonomics
  made its impact on design .

 During and right after WWII ("the knob and dial
  era"), much research was conducted to develop
  optimal design parameters for displays and controls .

 WWII triggered massive growth in HF/E :

       Aircraft accidents due to control/display confusion
       Birth of cockpit ergonomics and training design

 First time psychology and engineering were formally
  integrated .

Post - War Expansion (1950s                                              Ł1970s)

 Cold War era : HF/E used in aviation, nuclear power,
  and spaceflight (NASA) ...

 Birth of HFE societies :

       In 1949 , the Ergonomics Society was formed in Britain, and
         the first book on ergonomics was published .

       In the 1950 s, more people got involved, including
         academics, leading to research in non -military applications .

       In 1957 , the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society (HFES)
         was formed in the US .

 From 1960 and onwards, there was a rapid growth
  and expansion of ergonomics in the US .

 In 1970 , OSHA and NIOSH were both created by the
  US congress .

Occupational Safety and Health Administration
(OSHA)

 Since OSHA's establishment in 1971 ,
  occupational deaths have dropped by
  62 % and injuries by 42 % (OSHA
  website, 2009 ).

 OSHA currently provides ergonomic
  guidelines .

 OSHA can issue citations for ergonomic
  hazards under the General Duty Clause :

      Requires that each employer keep their
        workplaces free from recognized hazards
        that could likely cause death or serious
        physical harm .

Justifying an Ergonomics Program

1. Clearly define the problems

       Frequent accidents or near misses
       High medical and compensation costs
       Poor quality
       Low Productivity
       Frequent mistakes/errors
       High material waste
       Low job satisfaction
       High absenteeism
       Excessive labor turnover

2. Estimate the cost of the intervention

       Initial investment, including the labor to install the intervention.
       Maintenance and operation cost (if any)
       Time that workers will spend learning the system and/or training

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING                23
Justifying an Ergonomics Program

3. Use a validated ergonomic cost benefit analysis tool:

      Refer to OSHA website, society websites, Washing State
        Ergonomics

      Cost Benefit Analysis tool, ergonomic justification worksheets
        (Hedge, 2001).

4. Analyze the financial implications of the intervention

      Increase in productivity
      Reduction in workers' compensation costs
      Improvement in quality
      Consider results of interventions in other facilities (Case studies)
      Present results of pilot studies

5. Note the intangibles benefits:

 Compatibility of ergonomics with organizational goals

 Employee morale and job satisfaction

 Organization image

Sustaining the Effort

 For the ergonomic effort to turn into a long -term program:

      Develop directions for growth
      Develop a way for employees/managers to report accidents, near misses, concerns, and

        any other relevant data
      Adopt more of a proactive role
      Educate about ergonomics and safety
      Encourage everyone to be involved till ergonomics diffuses into the culture
      Keep track of cost savings
      Document success stories
      Surveys about job satisfaction
