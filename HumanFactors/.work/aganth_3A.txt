Importance in Engineering Design

 Designer should accommodate body dimensions of
  the population that will be using the equipment

 Universal operability is always desired; at least
  90% to 95% must be able to use the design

      Universal operability can be achieved by adjustable
        designs.

 Certain aspects of design may call for:

      Only one set of dimensions to be used.
      Consider 5th, 50th, and 95th percentiles.

Two Categories of Anthropometric                               Data

 Structural dimensions (static anthropometry)

      Taken with the body in various standard and still positions.
      Deals with simple dimensions of the stationary human being.
      E.g., weight, stature or height of the subject during standing, standing eye height, standing

        shoulder height, standing elbow height, chest depth, abdominal depth...

 Functional dimensions (dynamic anthropometry)

      Taken during physical activity (e.g., crawling height in mines)
      Photographic/videotaping methods.
      E.g., reach zones during standing, forward grip reach, vertical grip reach, side grip reach...

Static Anthropometry (Structural Dimensions)

                           Stature : With the subject standing erect and looking straight ahead, this is the
                           measure taken from the standing surface to the top of the head. Add 2.5 cm (1
                           in.) for men's shoes, 2 cm (0.8 in.) for caps, up to 8 cm (3.1 in.) for women's
                           shoes, and 3.8 cm (1.5 in.) for steel helmet.

Sitting Height : This is the vertical distance between the seat surface and the
top of the head with the subject sitting erect, looking straight ahead and the
knees at the right angles. For heavy clothing, add 1.2 cm (0.5 in.); add 2 cm (0.8
in.) for caps) and 3.8 cm (1.5 in.) for steel helmets.

Static Anthropometry (Structural Dimensions)

                             Knuckle Height : The vertical distance from the floor to the knuckle (typically
                             the third joint) with arms hanging naturally at the sides in a relaxed standing
                             position.

Acromial Height : The vertical distance from the floor to the acromion
process (the bony tip of the shoulder) when standing upright.

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING                                                 17
Static Anthropometry (Structural Dimensions)

                             Eye Height : The vertical distance from the floor to the inner corner of the
                             eye (canthus) when standing erect

Eye Height (Sitting) : The vertical distance from the sitting surface to the
inner corner of the eye while seated with erect posture.

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING                                              18
Static Anthropometry (Structural Dimensions)

                            Knee Height: Vertical distance is measured from the floor to the upper most
                            point on the knee. For light clothing and men's shoes, add 3 cm (1.2 in.); for boots
                            add 4.5 cm (3 in); for women shoes and light clothing, add up to 8 cm (3.1 in).

Popliteal Height: This is the vertical distance from the floor to the underside
of the thigh next to the knee with the subject sitting erect, knees at right
angles, and the bottom of the thigh and the back of the knee barely touching
the seat pan. Clothing adjustments are similar to knee height data.

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING  19
Static Anthropometry (Structural Dimensions)

                           Hip breadth, sitting : This is the maximum horizontal distance across the hips
                           with the subject sitting erect and knees together. Add 1.5 cm (0.6 in.) for light
                           clothing and 4 cm (1.6 in.) for heavy clothing.

Elbow -to-elbow breadth, sitting : This is the horizontal distance between the
lateral surfaces of the two elbows. The subject sits erect, hips and knees
together, upper arms vertical and touching the sides of the body lightly. Add 1.5
cm (0.6 in.) for light clothing and 12 cm (4.8 in.) or more for heavy clothing.

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING  20
Static Anthropometry (Structural Dimensions)

                             Arm reach: Horizontal distance is measured between the tip of the middle
                             finger and the posterior surface of the right shoulder. Subject stands erect with
                             ankles and knees together . The arm is extended to its maximum. Add 1.5 cm
                             (0.6 in.) for light clothing and light gloves. Add 2 cm (0.8 in.) or more for heavy
                             clothing and gloves. For manipulation by fingers, subtract 8 cm (3.2 in.); for
                             whole hand manipulation, subtract 12 cm (4.8 in.).

Elbow rest height (Sitting) : Vertical distance from the seat surface to the
underside of the elbow .

Static Anthropometry (Structural Dimensions)

                             Head length : This is the maximum length of the head from the most front to
                             the most rear of the head. Adjustment must be made due to the type of
                             head gear worn.

Thigh clearance (sitting) : This the vertical distance between the seat surface
and the point of intersection of the thigh with the abdomen. Add 0.5 cm for
light clothing and 3.5 cm or more for heavy clothing.

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING                                             22
Static Anthropometry (Structural Dimensions)

                      Hand length : This is the distance from the base of the thumb to the tip of the
                      middle finger of the right hand. The hand is extended straight on the arm. Add
                      0.4 cm (0.16 in.) for light gloves, and 0.8 cm (0.32 in.) or more for heavy gloves.

Foot length : This is the horizontal distance from the back of the heel to the tip
of the longest toe. Subject stands erect with his weight equally distributed on
both feet. Add 3 cm (1.2 in.) for men's shoes, 3.7 cm (1.5 in.) boots, and 2 cm
(0.8 in.) for women's shoes.

Chest Depth (Anterior ŁPosterior Thorax Depth) : The horizontal distance from the
most anterior point on the chest (typically the sternum) to the most posterior point
on the back (usually the spine/scapula) at the same vertical level.
