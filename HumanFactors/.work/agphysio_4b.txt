Energy Cost of Work

 What measures implicate an increase in physical work?

      Energy demands increase
      Oxygen consumption increases
      Heart rate increases
      Breathing rate accelerates
      Tidal volume increases
      Blood pressure increases
      Increased heat production causing sweating to dissipate heat

 Stress due to a physical activity can be evaluated using one or preferably a
  combination of the above objective measures

Muscle Fatigue

 Fatigue: reduction in performance ability due to
  excessive physical/mental demands followed by
  inadequate recovery time.

 Many factors affect muscle fatigue development:

      Strength
      External forces
      Posture
      Contraction time
      Recovery time

 Rohmert curve predicts that contractions below
  15 % MVC can be sustained indefinitely ( 10 min to
  > 1hr).

Strength

 It is estimated that for right -handed people, the strength of the left hand is
  approximately 10 % below that of the right hand.

 Strength is at its peak by the late twenties and shows a continuous decline
  afterwards. At age 65 , one has only 75 % of the strength of a youth (~ 25 -
  year -old).

 On average, women have two -thirds ( 66.67 %) the strength of men.

 Exercise can increase strength and endurance by as much as    50 %.

 Posture and duration are important factors.

Work - Rest Cycles

 An age allowance is necessary to consider after the RT calculation (as a multiplier).
 Research suggests that the most effective relief muscular fatigue is through small

  periods of rest taken several times during the duration of work rather than all taken
  at one time.

 It is customary in industry to allow for a 15 - to 20 -minute coffee break 2 hours
  after start in the morning and after lunch. These periods may be counted in the
  total rest duration.
