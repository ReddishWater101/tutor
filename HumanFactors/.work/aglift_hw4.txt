Part B: Exercises (76 pts)

   1. Define luminance, illuminance, and luminous flux. Provide the SI unit for each and
       one example relevant to human factors design (15 points).
            Luminous flux: total perceived power of light (unit: lumen, lm). Example: output
       of a light bulb.
            Illuminance: amount of light falling on a surface (unit: lux, lx). Example: office
       lighting levels.
            Luminance: light emitted/reflected from a surface in a given direction (unit: cd/m˛).
       Example: screen brightness.

   2. Why do older people need to hold a book farther away from them to read it if they do
       not use reading glasses? Explain in terms of age-related changes to the topics of the
       eye (10 points).

                                                          2
   As individuals age, the accommodation ability of their lens decreases. Accommodation
   is when the lens changes its shape to change the point of focus of the eye using the
   contraction and relaxation of the ciliary muscles. The goal of accommodation is the
   focus the image onto the back of the eye (i.e., the retina). If the image is not correctly
   focused on the retina then images would be blurry.

   Age related accommodation issues are due to decreased elasticity and increased hard-
   ness of the eye and changes in the strength of the ciliary muscles attached to the lens.
   These issues, also know as Presbyopia, cause the eye to focus light behind the retina
   for objects that are close. This causes objects that are close to appear blurry. The
   eyes do not need to accommodate as much for objects that are further away, so older
   people will hold objects further away to avoid the issues with accommodating close
   objects.

6. Describe three strategies you might use to address the problem of noise in a machine
   shop. Which one would you recommend for implementation over the other two and
   why? (10 points)
   Engineering controls (e.g., shielding, reducing noise intensity at source, adding panel-
   ing), Administrative controls (e.g., distance worker from source, reduce noise exposure
   duration), Personal protection (e.g., use aural and semi-aural inserts and circum-aural
   devices and thus hearing protection).

   Engineering controls would result in the best protection for workers since it will re-
   duce the intensity of the noise that the workers experience. Both personal protective
   equipment and administrative controls require worker compliance to be completely
   effective.
