Anatomy of the ear

 Outer ear:

       Pinna/Auricle (fleshy portion) and ear canal
       Modification of incoming pressure wave for localization
       Modification of pressure wave/acoustic wave

 Middle ear:

       Eardrum and ossicles (malleus, incus, and stapes)
       Conversion from airborne vibration to structural vibration
       Impedance matching of air vibration to fluid motion (in inner ear)

 Inner ear:

 Semi -circular canals, cochlear duct and organ of corti

 Transforms mechanical vibration to nerve impulse.

 Hair cells ( cilia ) move with fluid motion and generate neural signals

 Nerves at beginning of channel (base) respond to short wavelengths; high
   pitch sounds

 Nerves at end of channel (apex) respond to long wavelengths; low pitch
   sounds

Hearing Loss

 Masking:

       Perception of one sound affected by presence of another
         sound

       Alarms should be designed 15 dB above ambient noise
         levels (general "rule of thumb ") to ensure perception

 Temporary Threshold Shift (TTS):

       Temporary loss of sensitivity due to acute exposure to high
         amplitude signals

       e.g., "carryover " effect from using loud machines when
         trying to do something quieter

       e.g., leaving a loud concert and trying to have conversation

 Permanent Threshold Shift (PTS):

       Presbycusis Ł age -related hearing loss (primarily in high
         frequency)

       Sensorial -neural loss Ł hearing loss in high frequency range
         (damage to hair cells)

       Conductive loss Ł hearing loss due to earwax, ossification of
         bones of middle ear, or trauma (i.e., ruptured eardrum)

Combining sources and decibel calculation

 How do you calculate combined noise exposure when a person is
 exposed to more than one source simultaneously?

                           SPL Tot = 10 log 10 (= ())

 Formula requires division of dB measures by 10 before calculating
 power. Necessary for unit conversion.

 E.g., What is the combined (noise) level of two sounds of 86 and 96

decibels?

           = 10 log 10 (10 86 /10 + 10 96 /10 )

           = 96.4 dB

Psychophysical scaling of sound

 Perceived loudness is correlated with sound intensity:

 Perception is affected by both intensity and pitch

 Two sounds of equal intensity (e.g., 50 dB) but different in pitch (e.g., 500 Hz
  vs. 1000 Hz) are not of equal "loudness"

 Sone:

 Scale relating perceived / psychological experience of loudness to  physical
  intensity

 1 sone = loudness of sound at 40 dB and 1000 Hz (arbitrarily selected)

 Perceived loudness (sone) doubles with ~10 dB increase in intensity (holding
  pitch fixed)

 A phon is a unit of perceived loudness , tied to the decibel scale at 1000 Hz

Human factors application

 Intense sound and noise exposure must be controlled to prevent loss

       Normal conversation = 40 -60 dB
       Acute damage occurs at 140 dB (jet engine)
       e.g., operating jackhammer = 100 dB

 Many measures of protection exist:

       Engineering controls - Reduce noise intensity at source; interrupt noise in path
         (shielding); reduce reverberation/echo (paneling)

       Administrative controls Ł Distance worker from source; reduce noise exposure
         duration

       Personal protection Ł Use aural and semi -aural inserts and circum -aural devices
