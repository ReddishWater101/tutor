Manual Material Handling (MMH)

 Manual material handling (MMH) refers to a variety of techniques related to
  manually moving objects ( e.g. lift, lower, push, pull, carry, etc.).

 MMH is virtually everywhere, and associated injuries are high:

      25% of accidents in industry are related to MMH.
      60% of overexertion injuries are due to lifting/lowering.
      60% of low back pain (LBP) is primarily due to overexertion.
      LBP related to occupational factors costs $4.6 billion/yr.

MMH Research Approaches

 Evaluation of injury potential due to MMH follows four major avenues
 of research:

    1. Epidemiological approach : characterizes the job, workplace, and other
         factors to derive trends leading to injuries; main input: historical records.

    2. Biomechanical approach : characterizes forces/moments on the body to
         estimate task and load characteristics that may lead to injuries. Applicable
         for tasks where the frequency is less than 4 lifts per min.

    3. Psychophysical approach : participants adjust the load/task characteristics
         to arrive at an "acceptable " setup.

    4. Physiological approach : Evaluates MMH task in terms of cardiovascular
         stress, e.g. O2 consumption, HR, blood pressure, energy expenditure.

NIOSH Lifting Equation (1991 Revised)

 The NIOSH lifting equation was developed to help industry combat back injuries
  that result from lifting tasks.

      Identifies lifting tasks that may lead to back injuries
      Shows areas where task can be improved

 Originally developed in 1981 but revised in 1991.
 Equation computes a recommended weight limit (RWL) and compares it to

  actual weight used.

7. Coupling Multiplier (CM)

 Coupling (C): is the effectiveness of the hand container (or grip)
 interface, described as good, fair, or poor.

     `Good' if object allows hands to wrap around it (i.e. box with handles or hand
      hold cutouts).

     `Fair' if object has less than optimal handles or no handles but allows lifter to
      bend fingers at 90 o during lift.

     `Poor' if object is difficult to be grasped, with no handles or cutouts.

 0  CM  1.0

 CM is a function of C and V (Table 3.16 and 3.17)

Conditions of Use: NIOSH Lifting Equation

 There are situations where NIOSH lifting equation does NOT apply,
 such as:

     One handed -lift
     Duration > 8 hours
     Lifting while seated or in restricted workspace
     Lifting unstable objects
     Lifting while carrying, pushing, or pulling
     High speed motion (> 76 cm/s)
     Unreasonable floor/ foot coupling (< 0.4 coefficient of friction)
     Unfavorable environment (humidity and temperature)
