Main Effects vs. Interactions

 Main Effect: Effect of one IV on the DM, averaging across other IVs

      Think of it as asking: "If we only look at this one factor, does it make a difference?"

 Interaction Effect: Effect of one IV depends on the level of another IV

      It answers: "Does the impact of one factor change depending on the other factor?"

 Suppose you run an experiment on font size (small vs. large) and screen
  brightness (dim vs. bright) to see how they affect reaction time .

      A main effect of font size means that, on average, students read faster with large fonts
        compared to small fonts, regardless of screen brightness.

      A main effect of brightness means that, on average, students read faster on bright screens
        compared to dim ones, regardless of font size.

      Maybe large fonts only improve reaction time when the screen is dim but make no difference
        when the screen is bright.

      That means the effect of font size depends on brightness  that's an interaction.

Step 3: Conduct study

 Conduct pilot study to verify procedures and sample size

     Follow experiment plan (design of experiment) for 2 -3 subjects
     Modify procedures to prevent errors and systematic variance

       (uncontrolled factor exposure)
     Use pilot data (condition means and variances) to calculate sample

       size, given significance criterion and desired statistical power

 Conduct full experiment using refined procedure and verified
  sample size

Step 4: Analyze the data

 Before doing any analyses, look at descriptive statistics :

 Total number of participants (n); any missing data?

       May dictate appropriate

 Means, SD, histograms on DVs, trends of responses across levels of factors

 Visualize the data (boxplots, stem -and -leaf plots, etc.)

 Why do this work?

 Can develop idea of what data is telling you before conducting
   inferential statistics (look at trends).

 Apply appropriate inferential statistics

 t-test Ł simple single factor with two levels

 Simple linear regression Ł single factor data model with continuous predictor

 Multiple regression Ł multi -factor data model with continuous predictors

 Analysis of variance (ANOVA) Ł single and multi -factor data models including interaction
  effects

 Multivariate analyses Ł Analysis of effects on multiple responses accounting for potential correlations
   among responses

 Repeated measures analysis Ł Multiple observations on same experimental unit accounting for potential
   dependence of observations, use linear mixed models

Limitations in research

 Want "true" behavior of subjects we study:

 Observation necessarily leads to changes in behavior

 Persons under time study perform manual work ~12% faster (    Frievalds , 2013)

 Access to subjects may be limited in field research due to normal production operations

 Use of sample population:

       Want to make inferences on population behavior but don't have resources to collect data on every person
       Select individuals at random from population  sampling
       Each sample yields different group of individuals and variance in observed mean response

                Referred to as sampling error
                Smaller sample size Ł greater sampling error
                May need to conduct multiple experiments to estimate confidence interval on "true" mean

 Use of subjective response measures (e.g., surveys, ratings, interviews)

       Influenced by recall of events
       Influenced by performance bias (i.e., "workload" is often rated as low if task performance was "good")
       Can have limited face validity due to contamination Ł subjects may consider task demands other than those

         of interest to analyst

Challenges to HF/E research

 Limited funding

       May dictate sample size and sensitivity of analysis

 Limited time

       May dictate number of factor levels that can be tested and understanding of relationship
        between predictors and response (actual trend)

 Safety issues (in testing)

       Can lead to systematic error in responses (e.g., subject simulator sickness; subject
        perception of condition threat to well -being)

 What are some possible alternatives to circumvent ?

       Need to consider design options (within - vs. between -subjects).
       Need to consider experimental procedure options (necessary duration of test trials to

        observe effect)
