     Discovered in the 1990s

1. Vision adaptation (dark and light)

 Adaptation - Ability to see under
  different lighting conditions

 Going from light to dark

     Body suppresses cone threshold for
       active night vision

     Rods threshold also decreases ş
       Allows detection of small changes in
       illuminance and motion

     Takes about 30 min to dark adapt,
       (but just a few minutes to light adapt)

     Sensitivity to contrast among surfaces
       increases with dark adaptation

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING  17
1. Vision adaptation (dark and light)

 0-10 min in dark - Cones require less
  light to reach threshold response
  (perception of stimulus) than rods

 10+ min in dark - rods require less light
  for perception of stimulus.

 Point at which rods become more
  sensitive is called "rod-cone break".

 Glare can temporarily destroy rod cell
  sensitivity to low spatial frequencies
  (e.g., fail to detect dim road hazards due
  to water reflectance from headlights)

Human Factors Application

 Why are red lights used in cockpits?

      Rods and cones are NOT equally
        sensitive to all wavelengths

      Rods are insensitive to longer 
        (wavelength)

 Red light (low energy, long ) least
  detrimental to pilot night vision

      Rod cells are relatively insensitive to red
        light (receptors do not fatigue under red
        light); as if pilot had already spent time
        in dark

      Red light minimally triggers pupil
        constriction so pilot can continue to see
        dim lights outside of cockpit (e.g., other
        planes)

Human Factors Application

 Two problems with red light in
  cockpit:

    1. Human eye is less sensitive to
         appearance of red light than
         any other color of spectrum =
         poor reaction times

    2. Pilots cannot see red print
         (used on navigation charts)
         under red light:

            Cockpits have small, narrow-
              beam white light, used sparingly

            Pilots are instructed to close one
              eye when using white light
              sources to preserve night
              adaptation of eye.

2. Phorias

 Some people eyes converge too much, while others tend not to converge enough.
    Such conditions are called phorias.

 People with phorias also see one image but experience notable stress to the eye
    muscles, making them more vulnerable to rapidly developing visual fatigue.

3. Age

 With age, ciliary muscles weaken and visual performance declines.
 A young adult normally reads from a distance of about 30 cm (1 ft ). At 60, the

  near point of accommodation is around 83cm (2.75 ft).
 In industry, reading requirements, including character size, may be based on the

  limiting age of the expected user population.
