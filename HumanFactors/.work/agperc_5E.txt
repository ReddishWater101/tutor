Stimuli response

 Ergonomists use reaction speed to measure the ability of a person to perform
  psychomotor tasks.

 Reaction time (cognitive response): time that elapses from the appearance of a
  stimulus to the initiation of movement.

 Motion time (physical response): time that elapses during the actual movement.
 Response time (psychomotor response): reaction time + motion time

Mental Workload

 Mental workload is one of the most studied constructs from human factors
  engineering

 Mental workload: perception of effort or demands experienced in the
  performance of a cognitive task.

 Stress: condition or feeling that demands exceed resources to cope with those
  demands.

 Stress takes up working memory capacity thereby leaving fewer resources for
  processing, making it part of the total mental workload.

Mental Workload Measurement

 Mental workload can be determined using three different
 approaches or their combination:

1. Measures of primary and secondary task performance
2. Physiological measures
3. Subjective workload assessment

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING  8
1. Measures of primary and secondary task performance

 Primary task performance used as a measure to assess mental
 workload.

     Measures may include error rate, speed, reaction times, performance score
      etc.

 Secondary task performance is assumed to access the spare mental
 capacity that remains while focusing on the primary task. Poorer
 performance on secondary tasks implies a more mentally demanding
 primary task.

     Measures may include visual detection task, mental arithmetic, recalling
      information, etc.

2. Physiological measures

 Physiological measures include:

     Cardiac functions
     Eye movement (Eye tracking)
     Electromyography (EMG), i.e. muscle tension
     Electroencephalogram (EEG), i.e. brain activity
     Respiration
     Blood pressure
     Critical flicker fusion (CFF); 35 40 Hz at resting state; CFF drops 0.5 7 Hz under

       cognitive load.

 Generally, these measures are more difficult to collect and interpret
  because the results may not be exclusively affected by mental workload.

 These measures can supplement other workload assessment methods.

3. Subjective workload assessment

 The subject's direct estimate or comparative judgement of the mental or
  cognitive workload experienced at a given moment.

 Subjective assessments rely on questionnaires and feedback from employees to
  determine perceived workload.

 Three commonly used subjective workload assessment measurement
  approaches in human factors include:

     1. NASA Task Load Index (NASA TLX)
     2. Modified Cooper and Harper Scale (MCH)
     3. Overall Workload Scale (OW)

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING                  13
3. Subjective workload assessment: NASA                        - TLX

 NASA TLX: a subjective, multidimensional assessment tool for rating perceived workload.

 "Workload" is divided into six dimensions or
     1. Mental demand
     2. Physical demand
     3. Temporal demand
     4. Performance
     5. Effort
     6. Frustration

 Each scale consists of twenty steps ranging from 0 to 100 (ratings are assigned to
  the nearest 5).

3. Subjective workload assessment: NASA                        - TLX

 The task load index (TLX) is an overall workload score derived from the weighted
  average of the scale ratings.

 TLX score computation:

      1. Determine the individual weighting of each scale by asking participants to compare them pairwise in
          terms of their perceived importance/relevance to the task (a total of 15 pairwise comparisons).

      2. The total number of times each scale is chosen is the weighted score.
      3. Divide the weighted scores by 15 to get the relative importance of each scale.
      4. Multiply the weight of each scale to its corresponding score and add them together.

 Many researchers use the "Raw TLX" scores, without considering the overall TLX.

       i.e., individual scales may be dropped if less relevant to the task.

3. Subjective workload assessment: Overall Workload scale (OW)

 Overall workload: is a rating of the subject's overall workload on a
  unidimensional scale of 0 to 100 (assigned to the nearest 5).

      0 = very low workload
      100 = very high workload

 A single, 20 step bipolar scale is used to obtain this global rating.
 "The total workload associated with the task considering all sources and

  components."

Comparing the Three Techniques

 MCH and OW are unidimensional workload scales, which take less time to collect
  and analyze the data.

 TLX is a multidimensional scale, which entail special sorting procedures that
  take more time, but are justified by the detailed, diagnostic information that they
  provide.

 TLX and OW are consistently superior in terms of sensitivity and have the
  strongest operator acceptance.
