What is Evidence?

 Now that we looked at design methods.. We need to support it.
 Evidence = Data that supports design decisions
 Can be qualitative (observations, interviews) or quantitative (task time, error rates)
 Helps answer:

      Does the design meet user needs?
      Is it usable, safe, and efficient?
      What should be improved?

Purpose of Evaluation

 To ensure systems are usable, safe, and effective
 To identify and fix design flaws before deployment
 To understand user needs, behavior, and context
 Design evaluation

      Testing specific prototypes, design solutions, or systems
      e.g., apple watch vs. in -vehicle infotainment systems

 Concept evaluation
     Basic science that supports the design principles and heuristics
     e.g., visual distraction, cognitive distractions, manual distractions

Types of Evaluation: Formative VS Summative

Formative Evaluation:
 Conducted during design
 Helps improve the design
 Involves early feedback
 Diagnose problems with prototypes
 Qualitative measures
 Less formal than summative evaluations
 FDA:

      "Formative evaluations are used to inform device user interface
      design while it is in development. It should focus on the issues
      that the preliminary analyses indicated were most likely to
      involve use safety (e.g., aspects of user interaction with the
      device that are complicated and need to be explored). It should
      also focus on those areas where design options for the user
      interface are not yet final."

FDA Guidelines: "Applying Human Factors and Usability Engineering to Medical Devices"

Types of Evaluation: Formative VS Summative

Summative Evaluation:
 Assess whether a design meets benchmarks or design goals
 Conducted after development
 Verify and validate
 Quantitative measures (measure an underlying variable Ł

   Annett's Evaluative Methods)
 Measures usability, performance, satisfaction
 Supports decision -making (e.g., go/no -go)

 FDA: Human factors validation testing

       "Human factors validation testing is conducted to demonstrate that the
       device can be used by the intended users without serious use errors or
       problems , for the intended uses and under the expected use
       conditions . The testing should be comprehensive in scope, adequately
       sensitive to capture use errors caused by the design of the user
       interface and should be performed such that the results can be
       generalized to actual use."

FDA Guidelines: "Applying Human Factors and Usability Engineering to Medical Devices"

Heuristic Evaluation Process

1. Identify and select human factors principles that are particularly applicable.
2. Carefully inspect the design and identify where it violates the design

     principles.
3. Aggregate findings for each evaluator and communicate to design team.

Cognitive Walkthrough

 Similar to heuristic evaluation

      Expert evaluation
      Highlight potential problems with the design

 Take on perspective of user

      Step -by-step analysis of how users solve tasks

 Requires a specific set of tasks/use cases
 Best suited for interaction design
 Focus on learnability for new or infrequent users
 Ask: "Will the user know what to do next?"
 Useful for interfaces with complex workflows

Cognitive Walkthrough

 Is it likely that the person will perform
  the right action?

  perform the task?

In - Service Evaluation

 In -service evaluation refers to the ongoing assessment of a system, product, or
  process after it has been deployed and is in actual use -- i.e., it's already in service.

 Summative system assessment

 Ideally done within real -world contexts

 Controlled experiments

     Compare new system versus status quo (e.g., clinical trial)
     Compare measures of interest against benchmarks

 Examples:

     Monitoring nurse interaction with electronic health records months after implementation
     Gathering feedback from pilots on a new interface after flight hours accumulate
     Evaluating the maintenance logs and downtime of a newly installed machine in a factory

A/B Testing

 Also known as split -testing

 Two systems (or more; e.g., webpages) evaluated
  against each other

 Small change (e.g., the headline) and test conversion
  rate (people who sign up or click through).

 Very large number of participants typically

       Facebook / Google

 Can be used to test very small differences (slightly
  different shades of blue)

 Best for minor design variations, not big conceptual
  differences

 Doesn't explain why one version worked better                 -- just
  which one did
