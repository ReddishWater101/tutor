Predetermined motion time system (PMTS)

 Human terms: Break down a job into its most basic parts and then add the time for each
  part based on predetermined time estimates

 PMTS is a work measurement technique whereby times established for basic human
  motions are used to build up the time for a job at a defined level of performance.

 PMTS also called predetermined time system (PTS) , is a database of basic motion
  elements and their associated normal time values, together with a set of procedures for
  applying the data to analyze manual tasks and establish standard times for the tasks.

 There are some assumption for PMTS which are independent and additive; that is, each
  element does not affect what happens before or after it.

MTM - 1

 MTM breaks down all basic motions into units of               TMU (time -measurement unit)

      1 TMU = 0.00001 hr
      1 TMU = 0.0006 min
      1 TMU = 0.036 sec
      1 hour = 100,000 TMU
      1 min = 1667 TMU
      1 sec = 27.8 TMU

 Times are for a trained (normal) worker working at a normal pace.

 No allowances are included in the times.

 Steps for determining standard time using MTM -1

    1. Break down the left - and right -hand motions into steps
    2. Break down the steps into sequences of the basic motions
    3. Assign the appropriate symbol for each basic motion
    4. Find the equivalent TMUs from the MTM -1 tables
    5. Remove the non -limiting values
    6. Calculate standard time

Reach

 Reach involves moving the hand or fingers to a definite destination or to a general
  location. It depends on:

      Distance
      Type of reach

              A. Reach to object in fixed location, or to object in other hand, or to object on which other hand rests.
              B. Reach to single object in location that may vary slightly from cycle to cycle.
              C. Reach to object jumbled with other objects, requiring search and select as well.
              D. Reach to a very small object, or an object for which accurate grasp is required.
              E. Reach to indefinite location to get hand in position for body balance, or for next motion, or out of the way.

 Example: Reaching an object jumbles with others objects in group from 1 inch distance.

Grasp

 Gain control of an object; often followed by Move.

     1. Pick up grasp: grasp an individual object

             A. By itself (most common)
             B. Small object lying against the surface (like a small paper)
             C. Cylindrical objects (like pens or markers next to each other)

     2. Regrasp: change grasp without relinquishing control; object stays in your hand
     3. Transfer: control transferred from one hand to the other
     4. Select: objects jumbled with each other such that search and select often occur

             A. Large
             B. Medium
             C. Small

     5. Contact: contact or sliding grasp (like pressing a button); your fingers to do not really hold
          on to anything

Position

 Align, orient, and engage one object with another object. Depends on:

       Class of fit (loose, close, exact):

               · Loose: like a cup to its saucer
               · Close: like a marker cap to a marker
               · Exact: like a key to its keyhole

       Symmetry (symmetrical, semi -symmetrical, non - symmetrical)

               · Symmetrical: infinity of ways to insert the object (like a pen inside a cup)
               · Semi -symmetrical: finite ways to insert the object (like a paper into a rectangular cardboard box)
               · Non -symmetrical: one and only one way to insert the object (like a key inside a keyhole)

       Ease of handling: how easy the object is to hold (slippery, dangerous, sharp...)
