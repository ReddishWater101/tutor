What is "Human Factors and Ergonomics"?

From the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society (HFES):

 Ergonomics (or human factors) is the scientific discipline concerned with the            , and
  understanding of interactions among humans and other elements of a system
  the profession that applies theory, principles, data and methods to design in order
  to optimize human well -being and overall system performance . 

 Ergonomists contribute to the design and evaluation of tasks, jobs, products,
  environments and systems to make them compatible with the needs, abilities and
  limitations of people.

Basic Ergonomic model

 The Ergonomic model is also known as the human -in-the -loop model.

 The human -machine system includes one or more humans and machines (or tools)
  in a given environment performing one or more tasks to achieve one or more goals.

 The human -computer interaction includes interfaces between human and
  computers.

Goals & Standards

 Goals should describe:

     The reason(s) that the system exist
     Why the task is performed -- not how.
     The expected work/task performance

            There may be multiple goals
            There may be short -term and long -term goals
            Workers, employers, and consumers all may have different goals
            Goals may be explicitly stated or implied

 Work standards describe:

     Quantity, quality, and time for achieving the goals
     Standard is set by an employer, equipment manufacturer, and worker/user

            Methods, equipment/tools, workspace, environment, Materials
            Worker/user qualifications, training, special needs

Classification of Ergonomic Problems

 Ergonomic problems can be classified into the following broad (and sometimes
    overlapping) categories:

1. Anthropometric
2. Musculoskeletal
3. Cardiovascular
4. Cognitive
5. Psychomotor
6. Organizational

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING  24
Classification of Ergonomic Problems

1. Anthropometric

       Study of human body dimensions and body proportions. Joints' range of
            motion and strength norms are also considered in anthropometrics

       E.g., reach distances, sitting eye height, also includes weight and volume
       Application can reduce the dimensional conflict between functional space

            geometry and the human body

2. Musculoskeletal

       Tasks that strain the muscular and skeletal systems are in this category
       E.g., overexertion injuries, posture analysis, repetitive tasks

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING                      25
Classification of Ergonomic Problems

3. Cardiovascular

       Tasks that stress the heart and circulatory system

       E.g., heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen consumption, energy expenditure

       When a demand is placed on the cardiovascular system, the heart pumps
            more blood to the muscles to meet the elevated oxygen requirements.

       If the activity is excessive, it can lead to fatigue, exhaustion, dehydration,
            and produce both short - and long -term negative effects

4. Cognitive

 Related to either information -processing overload or underload.

 These functions should be sufficiently utilized for the maintenance of an
      optimum state of awareness

 Tasks should be designed to be compatible with cognitive capabilities
      and to complement human functions with machine functions for
      enhanced performance

UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA HERBERT WERTHEIM COLLEGE OF ENGINEERING                          26
Classification of Ergonomic Problems

5. Psychomotor

       Problems that strain the systems that respond to visual or
            auditory stimuli with movement or muscular activity.

       E.g., paced manipulative work with significant visual demands,
            surgical procedure, sewing task

6. Organizational

       Problems dealing with the organizational structures, policies,
            and processes in the work environment.

       E.g., design of shift hours, communication, job satisfaction,
            motivation, supervision, teamwork, ethics, and culture
