# Practice Final Narrative — Human Factors and Ergonomics (EIN3241)

## Part One: Multiple Choice

**Question One.** WWII trigger for HF/E expansion — answer C, frequent human errors due to poorly designed military equipment. Pilots in working aircraft were crashing because controls looked alike and dials were misread, so psychologists and engineers teamed up to redesign cockpits. The keyword pair is human error plus military equipment.

**Question Two.** Standard time definition — answer C. The phrase qualified worker, standard method, normal conditions is the textbook definition. The trap is normal time, which is the input that gets inflated by the allowance to become standard time.

**Question Three.** Best job for time study — answer B, manual labor and repetitive cycles. You need the human to be the bottleneck (manual) and the work to repeat enough cycles to sample (repetitive).

**Question Four.** Westinghouse rating, not a factor — answer D, posture. The four Westinghouse factors are skill, effort, conditions, consistency. Posture sounds ergonomic but is not on the list.

**Question Five.** Allowances exclude — answer D, normal time. Allowances cover fatigue, personal needs, unavoidable delays, and setup. Normal time is the thing being inflated, not a category of inflation.

**Question Six.** One TMU equals — answer C, zero point zero three six seconds. To convert TMU to seconds, multiply by zero point zero three six.

**Question Seven.** MTM element for rotating the hand — answer D, Turn. Reach is to a target, Move relocates an object, Turn rotates the hand and wrist.

**Question Eight.** Important displays attract attention — answer C, salience compatibility. The visual or auditory weight of a signal should match its importance.

**Question Nine.** Fifteen dB above ambient overcomes — answer A, pitch masking. Sounds at similar frequencies interfere on the cochlea; the fifteen dB margin punches the alarm through.

**Question Ten.** Redundancy gain — answer C, present information using multiple cues. Traffic lights use color and position so colorblind drivers still read them.

**Question Eleven.** Information access cost — answer B, frequently used items easy to reach. Save costly attention shifts for rarely used information.

**Question Twelve.** Avoid absolute judgment — answer C, limit the number of coded levels. Channel capacity is about seven plus or minus two per dimension; beyond that, working memory saturates.

**Question Thirteen.** Peripheral vision wider in — answer B, horizontal plane. About two hundred eight degrees horizontal versus one hundred sixty degrees vertical, due to lateral eye muscles and the brow-and-cheek bone clipping vertical reach.

**Question Fourteen.** Poor coupling — answer B, object difficult to grasp with no handles or cutouts. Pulls the coupling multiplier down to zero point nine in the NIOSH equation.

**Question Fifteen.** Fatigued radiologist — answer B, lower sensitivity and increased misses. Fatigue blurs the perceptual distributions, so d-prime drops. This is a sensitivity change, not a bias change.

**Question Sixteen.** Fitts' Law longest movement time — answer B, small and far. Both shrinking W and growing A push movement time up.

**Question Seventeen.** A/B testing limit — answer C, identifies which design is better but not why. It is a quantitative winner-picker without causal mechanism.

**Question Eighteen.** Reach distances and body dimensions — answer C, anthropometric. Anytime a question is about whether the body fits or reaches, it is anthropometric.

**Question Nineteen.** ROC curves, same hits, lower P false alarm — answer A, higher d-prime. Beta shifts move you along a single curve; only different sensitivity puts you on a different, better-bowed curve.

**Question Twenty.** Seated work advantage — answer B, improved blood circulation and less fatigue. The other options all describe standing.

## Part Two: Short Answer

**Short Answer One — Drinking fountain, non-adjustable.** For the control button, use the fifth percentile of arm reach. This is a reach problem, so design for the small extreme — if the smallest can reach, everyone can. For the spout, use the fiftieth percentile of standing elbow height. This is a comfort problem with no extreme in danger, so design for the average. Mantra: fifth for reach, ninety-fifth for clearance, fiftieth for comfort.

**Short Answer Two — Wrench handle.** Hand breadth has mean ten centimeters and standard deviation one point eight centimeters. A handle is a fit problem — the largest hand drives the design — so use the ninety-fifth percentile. Equation sheet page one, Percentile section: x sub p equals m plus z sub p times SD. The z value for the ninety-fifth percentile is one point six four five. Plug in: handle length L equals ten plus one point six four five times one point eight. Do the multiplication, then the addition. Answer is in centimeters.

**Short Answer Three — Standing work surface.** Rule of thumb is five to ten centimeters below elbow height — the light-work default. Precision work goes five to ten above; heavy work goes fifteen to forty below.

**Short Answer Four — Psychophysical approach.** Workers themselves adjust load, frequency, and conditions until they perceive the setup as acceptable. The output is a set of perceived limits, which feed safety guidelines and the original Snook–Ciriello tables that became part of NIOSH.

**Short Answer Five — Kids' workstation, seven problems.** Pick from: monitor too high (neck extension), keyboard too high (shoulder elevation), non-adjustable chair, no lumbar support, excessive seat depth so the back cannot reach the backrest, no footrest with feet dangling, viewing distance too close, no arm support, no seat cushion. Unifying lesson: smaller does not equal ergonomic. Adjustability and proportion to the actual user are what matter.

## Part Three: Problem One — MTM-One Stapler Task

Worker stands at a table. Papers on one side, stapler buried in a basket of mixed items on the other side, drawer below. Right hand does most of the work; left hand puts the papers in the stapler.

Use the PMTS Tables on equation sheet pages seven through ten. For each motion, ask three questions: what category — Reach, Move, Grasp, Position, Apply Pressure, Release, body — what case — for Reach, A through E; for Move, A through C; for Grasp, G1 through G5; for Position, fit class with symmetry and handling — and what distance, weight, or angle.

Get the stapler: R-twenty-C because it is jumbled with other items, then G4-B because select-grasp on a medium object, then M-twelve-B back to the workspace with a weight factor for a roughly three-pound stapler.

Align stapler exactly: P3SSE — exact fit, semi-symmetrical, easy handling.

Staple: APB, apply pressure with precision since you just positioned.

Open drawer: Bend (twenty-nine TMU body motion in the right hand column), R-A to handle, G1A pickup, M-B pull out, RL1 release.

Put stapler in drawer: R-B to stapler, G1A, M-B with weight factor, RL1.

Close drawer: R-A, contact, M-B, release. Then Arise from bend, thirty-one point nine TMU.

Left hand: R-B to papers, G1B for thin object on a surface, M to stapler, position if needed, RL1.

No simultaneous motions, so add everything sequentially. The total comes to two hundred forty-seven point eight TMU. Multiply by zero point zero three six to convert to seconds. State your distance and weight assumptions below the table.

## Part Three: Problem Two — Time Study

Three elements over five cycles. Element three only happens in cycles two and five with values four and three; the dashes mean it was skipped. Rating one hundred percent, allowance nine percent, fourteen pieces in eight hours, ten dollars per hour, standard hour plan.

Equation sheet page five, Time Studies section. Average each element by dividing by the number of times it actually occurred — element three's average is the sum of four and three divided by two, not by five.

Identify frequencies: f-one is five, f-two is five, f-three is two. The equation sheet warns that f-one must be the highest frequency.

Combine using ET equals ET-one plus the ratio f-two over f-one times ET-two, plus the ratio f-three over f-one times ET-three. So ET equals twenty-one point four plus one times eighteen point six, plus two-fifths times three point five.

Apply rating: NT equals ET times R over one hundred. R is one hundred so NT equals ET.

Apply allowance: ST equals NT times the quantity one plus zero point zero nine, which is NT times one point zero nine.

Earned hours: fourteen pieces times ST per piece, divided by sixty.

Efficiency: earned hours divided by eight actual hours, times one hundred for percent.

Direct labor cost: earned hours times ten dollars per hour. Standard hour plan pays for what was earned, not for clock time, so an efficient worker earns more than they would on hourly.

## Part Three: Problem Three — Noise Dose and TWA

Three machines: A at eighty-eight dB, B at eighty-seven dB, C at eighty-six dB. Exposures five, two, and one hours.

Equation sheet page four. Permissible exposure limit per machine: L sub T equals eight divided by two raised to the quantity L minus ninety, all over five. Plug in eighty-eight, eighty-seven, eighty-six one at a time. All below ninety, so each L-T comes out greater than eight hours.

Noise dose: D equals the sum of T-i divided by L-T-i. So five over L-T-A, plus two over L-T-B, plus one over L-T-C. Dose is unitless.

Time-weighted average: TWA equals sixteen point six one times log base ten of D, plus ninety. Result in dBA. By design, D equals one corresponds to TWA equals ninety, the OSHA limit.

Compare to ninety dBA. If your TWA is below ninety, the worker is under the limit.

Watch out for: subtracting ninety in the L-T exponent (the calibration), keeping the five-dB OSHA exchange (not the three-dB NIOSH version), and not adding decibels linearly — that would be a different problem.

## Part Three: Problem Four — Fitts' Law

Equation sheet page four. MT equals a plus b times log base two of the quantity two A divided by W. Index of Difficulty is the log piece alone.

For each row, compute two A divided by W, take log base two, round to the nearest integer.

Row one: A is eight, W is four. Two A over W is four. Log base two of four is exactly two.

Row two: A fifteen, W four. Ratio seven point five. Log base two roughly two point nine, rounds to three.

Row three: A eighteen, W two. Ratio eighteen. Log base two roughly four point one seven, rounds to four.

Row four: A twenty, W one point five. Ratio twenty-six point seven. Log base two roughly four point seven four, rounds to five.

Row five: A thirty, W one. Ratio sixty. Log base two roughly five point nine one, rounds to six.

So the IDs are two, three, four, five, six. Calculator tip: log base two of x equals log base ten of x divided by zero point three zero one.

Three operators. Operator one: MT equals zero point two plus zero point seven times ID. Operator two: MT equals one plus two times ID. Operator three: MT equals two plus three times ID.

Operator one wins on both intercept and slope, so for any positive ID, Operator one's movement time is the smallest. Most productive, shortest movement times — both because of lower base time and lower difficulty penalty.

Two suggestions to reduce movement time: decrease A by moving targets closer, or increase W by making targets larger. Both shrink ID inside the log.

Quick intuition for log base two: each bit halves uncertainty, so doubling the A-over-W ratio adds exactly one bit of difficulty.

## Part Three: Problem Five — Signal Detection

Twenty trials, ten cold-brew (signal), ten regular iced (noise). Customer said cold-brew eleven times; nine were correct.

Translate to counts. Hits equal nine. False alarms equal two — the other yes-responses, the ones on noise trials. Misses equal one. Correct rejections equal eight. Watch the trap: eleven yes-responses is not eleven hits — it is hits plus false alarms.

Convert to rates by dividing by row totals (ten signal, ten noise). Hit rate zero point nine. Miss rate zero point one. False alarm rate zero point two. Correct rejection rate zero point eight.

Equation sheet page four for d-prime: capital Z of P-of-H minus capital Z of P-of-FA. Look up z-scores in the Normal and Ordinates Tables on page six.

Z of zero point nine is one point two eight. Z of zero point two is negative zero point eight four — the negative is critical because z below zero point five is negative.

Plug into d-prime: one point two eight minus negative zero point eight four. That is one point two eight plus zero point eight four. Add it.

For beta, primary form: ordinate of Z of H over ordinate of Z of FA. Ordinate at one point two eight is zero point one seven five eight. Ordinate at negative zero point eight four is zero point two eight zero three — ordinate is symmetric, same as at positive zero point eight four. So beta equals zero point one seven five eight divided by zero point two eight zero three. Divide.

Beta less than one means liberal, greater than one means conservative, equal to one means neutral.

## Part Three: Problem Six — NIOSH Lifting Index

Tote box, fifteen pounds, three lifts per minute, eight hours, good coupling, H equals sixteen inches, V equals forty-four, V-two equals sixty-two, A equals eighty degrees.

Equation sheet page five for LI: LI equals L over RWL.

For RWL, equation sheet page eleven: RWL equals LC times HM times VM times DM times AM times FM times CM. US units throughout because pounds and inches.

LC is fifty-one pounds.

HM equals ten over H, so ten over sixteen.

VM equals one minus zero point zero zero seven five times the absolute value of V minus thirty. So one minus zero point zero zero seven five times the absolute value of forty-four minus thirty, which is fourteen.

DM equals zero point eight two plus one point eight over D. D equals V-two minus V equals sixty-two minus forty-four equals eighteen. So zero point eight two plus one point eight over eighteen.

AM equals one minus zero point zero zero three two times A. So one minus zero point zero zero three two times eighty.

FM from Table two on page eleven: three lifts per minute, greater-than-two-but-less-than-or-equal-to-eight-hours duration, V at or above thirty. Reads zero point five five.

CM from Table three on page twelve: good coupling, V at or above thirty. Reads one point zero zero.

Multiply all seven for RWL. Then LI equals fifteen divided by RWL.

Interpret: LI under one is safe, LI over one means redesign needed, LI over three means urgent. This task comes out a bit over one, so the conclusion is the job needs redesign — bring the load closer, eliminate the eighty-degree twist, slow the pace, improve coupling, or lighten the load.

Watch out: use US-unit formulas (the metric ones have different constants), keep the absolute value in VM, and remember V-two only matters as part of D.
