## Investment Decision Problems (Break Even Analysis)

This is an "is this investment worth it?" problem. Imagine you have a chunk of money and someone is trying to sell you on spending it on something big (a machine, a robot, a new piece of equipment) that promises to make you more money over time. The question is basically: "Should I actually do this, or would I be better off just sticking my money somewhere safe and boring?" To answer that, you compare the upfront cost against the extra money it brings in over its lifetime, while keeping in mind that a dollar earned five years from now is worth less than a dollar in your pocket today.

To make the decision, you usually look at the situation from three different angles. First, you ask "how much extra do I get back compared to what I put in?" (a percentage style answer). Second, you ask "how long until this thing pays for itself?" (a time style answer, like "two and a half years"). Third, you ask "if I add up all the future money it earns and shrink it down into today's dollars, is it actually worth more than what I paid?" Each angle tells you something a little different, and together they give you a well rounded gut check on whether the shiny new thing is actually a smart buy or just expensive hype.

## ROI (Return on Investment)

ROI is the "bang for your buck" number. Imagine you hand over a chunk of money for something, and over time it earns you some profit. ROI just asks: out of every dollar you spent, how much extra did you make on top? You take all the profit you got back, subtract what you originally paid, and then divide that by what you paid. The result is usually a percentage, like "I got 26 percent more back than I put in." A bigger ROI means the investment was more efficient at turning your money into more money. It is the simplest score to compare two options head to head, but it has a blind spot: it does not care whether you earned that profit in one year or twenty, which is why we need the other two tools.

## PBP (Payback Period) and NPV (Net Present Value)

PBP answers a much more impatient question: "how long until I get my money back?" You start in the hole by however much the investment cost, and each year you chip away at that hole with the profits the investment brings in. The moment the hole is filled, that is your payback period, usually measured in years (and often a fractional year, like 3.96 years). Shorter is better, because the sooner you break even, the less time your money is sitting at risk. NPV is the most "honest" of the three because it remembers that money today is worth more than money later (you could have invested it elsewhere and earned interest). So instead of just adding up future profits at face value, you shrink each future year's profit down to what it would be worth in today's dollars, add them all up, and then subtract the upfront cost. If the final number is positive, the investment beats just sticking your money in a safe interest bearing account; if it is negative, you would have been better off doing nothing fancy.
