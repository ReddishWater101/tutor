1. What is the (q,r) Policy?

You continuously watch your inventory. When it drops to r units, you order q units. That's the whole rule.

The Inventory Cycle - Interactive

Drag the orange slider below the chart to change the reorder point r. Watch how it affects stockouts.


Key Concepts

q (order quantity): How many units you order each time. The vertical jump up on the chart.

r (reorder point): The inventory level that triggers a new order. The horizontal dashed line.

Lead time (τ): The delay between placing and receiving an order. Demand keeps happening during this time.

Safety stock (s): The expected inventory left when the order arrives. s = r - expected demand during lead time.

n(r): Expected number of units short per cycle. Higher r = fewer shortages.

Why not just use EOQ or Newsvendor?

EOQ assumes demand is known and constant. Newsvendor assumes no fixed ordering cost and instant delivery. Real life has both random demand AND a fixed cost to order AND a delivery delay. The (q,r) policy handles all three.

2. Demand During Lead Time (DDLT)

While you wait for your order, customers keep buying. DDLT = total demand during that wait. It's random.

Constant vs Random Lead Time

Constant Lead Time (σ_τ = 0)

σ_L² = μ_τ × σ_D² + μ_D² × σ_τ²

Second term drops out! Less uncertainty.

Random Lead Time (σ_τ > 0)

σ_L² = μ_τ × σ_D² + μ_D² × σ_τ²

Both terms active. Much more uncertainty!

What is n(r)?

n(r) = expected shortages per cycle. It's the average amount demand overshoots your reorder point during lead time. The shaded orange area above. Higher r = less shaded area = fewer shortages.

When demand is normal: n(r) = σ_L × L(z), where z = (r - μ_L)/σ_L

4. Full Backlogging vs Lost Sales

Backlogging = customer waits. Lost sales = customer leaves forever. This changes the formulas.

Full Backlogging

Customer says "I'll wait." Demand carries over.

Reorder condition:

1 - F(r*) = qh / (bλ)

Safety stock:

s = r - E[X_L]

Intuition: Shortages aren't as catastrophic because you'll fill them later. So r can be a bit lower.

Lost Sales

Customer leaves. Sale gone forever.

Reorder condition:

1 - F(r*) = qh / (qh + bλ)

Safety stock:

s = r - E[X_L] + n(r)

Intuition: Losing sales is worse, so r is pushed higher. Also inventory never goes negative (unsold demand vanishes), so safety stock includes n(r).

Both use the same iterative approach and the same q* equation. The only differences are the r equation denominator and the safety stock formula.

5. The Iterative Approach

Guess q, find r, find shortages, update q, repeat until nothing changes.

How it works (the loop)

1. Start: q₀ = √(2Kλ/h)   ← pretend no shortages
2. Plug q into reorder equation → get F(r) → look up z → round down to 2 decimals
3. Same z row → read L(z) → calculate n(r) = σ_L × L(z)
4. Plug n(r) into q equation → get new q
5. If z changed → go to step 2. If same → DONE.

6. Service Levels

When you can't estimate shortage cost b, use a service level target instead. Two types exist.

Type I - Cycle Service Level (α)

"What's the probability I don't stock out in a cycle?"

It's pass/fail per cycle. Either you had enough or you didn't.

F(r) = α
q = √(2Kλ/h)

q and r are independent. No iteration needed!

99% Type I → very high r because you need to almost never stock out.

Type II - Fill Rate (β)

"What fraction of units demanded do I fill from stock?"

It's proportional. Even if you stock out, maybe you only missed 3 units out of 2000.

n(r) = (1-β) × q
q = √(2Kλ/h)  (approximate)

Also no iteration for approximate version!

99% Type II → much lower r because filling 99% of units is easier than never stocking out.

Why does the same % give different answers?

At 99%, Type I says: "In 99 out of 100 cycles, I must have zero stockouts." That's extremely strict.

Type II says: "Out of every 100 units demanded, I fill 99 from stock." You can have small stockouts and still hit 99%.

That's why Type I always gives a higher reorder point for the same percentage.
